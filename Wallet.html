Kroon Community Voucher Wallet

Background

Financial exclusion is a significant issue in South Africa, particularly for low-income communities and micro-entrepreneurs. Traditional banking systems often exclude these groups due to high service costs and stringent requirements. In response, community currencies and voucher-based systems have emerged as alternatives that enable individuals to participate in local economies without relying on conventional financial institutions.

The Kroon Voucher, co-designed with the Ocean View community in South Africa, is an example of a community currency integrated into a digital wallet. This project aimed to develop a user-friendly, low-barrier solution for financial transactions within this community. The wallet is part of the broader iNethiApp, a platform designed to support local content services and improve internet accessibility in Ocean View. The community was deeply involved in the design process through workshops, ensuring that the final product aligned with their unique financial needs and technological limitations.

Aims and Objectives

Objective 1
Develop a community voucher digital wallet

Design and implement a digital wallet system tailored for the Kroon community voucher, enabling local transactions and economic participation.

Objective 2
Ensure usability through co-design

Engage community members in the design process to create an intuitive and user-friendly digital wallet that meets their specific needs and preferences.

Objective 3
Address local concerns

Develop features that minimize data usage, ensure accessibility, and cater to users with limited connectivity and financial literacy.

Methodology

This research utilized a co-design approach to ensure that the Kroon wallet met the specific needs of the Ocean View community. Co-design is a participatory methodology where the end-users—in this case, community members—are involved in the design and development process. This method ensured that the digital wallet was both usable and relevant to the target audience, who are often excluded from traditional financial systems.

Workshop 1: Understanding Community Needs

This initial workshop gathered insights on the community's financial practices and challenges, particularly focusing on their interaction with local services and access to financial tools. Participants shared their preferences and pain points in using both digital and traditional financial services.

Workshop 2: Feature Ranking and Prioritization

In this phase, participants ranked different features of the digital wallet based on their importance. The session focused on understanding which functionalities—such as balance checking, security protocols, and voucher transfers—were most critical to users. Feedback was collected on the design, ensuring that it remained simple and accessible.

Workshop 3: App Testing and Feedback

The final workshop involved participants testing the latest version of the Kroon wallet. Cognitive walkthroughs were performed to observe how users interacted with the app and identify any usability challenges. The feedback from this session informed further refinements to the wallet, focusing on improving user experience and addressing technical issues like data usage.

Results

The co-design workshops led to several key insights that shaped the final version of the Kroon wallet:

  • Feature Preferences: Community members prioritized basic functionalities such as checking balances and transferring vouchers. More advanced features, like earning additional vouchers, were deprioritized in favor of simplicity.
  • Security: While the community did not see a strong need for extensive security features, they requested a PIN-based authentication system for voucher transactions. This satisfied their need for basic security without over-complicating the user experience.
  • Data Usage: One of the most critical needs identified was minimizing data usage, as the high cost of internet access was a major barrier for many users. The wallet was developed to function in low-data environments, with offline capabilities being explored.
  • Onboarding Challenges: While the app was considered easy to use, the onboarding process was a significant pain point, with participants struggling to complete account setup without external help.

Conclusions

The Kroon voucher digital wallet successfully addressed key issues of financial exclusion by providing a simple, user-centered solution for local transactions. The co-design process ensured that the community's unique needs were met, leading to a product that was intuitive and aligned with their daily financial behaviors. The workshops highlighted the importance of user engagement in design, particularly in contexts where digital literacy is low.

Despite the app's success in terms of core functionality, onboarding remains an area for improvement. Participants found it difficult to set up accounts independently, suggesting the need for a clearer, more streamlined onboarding process.

Security was not a significant concern for the community, reinforcing the idea that financial tools should be designed based on users' perceptions of risk. The decision to include minimal security (e.g., a simple PIN for transactions) reflects this insight.

Future Work

Future work will focus on improving the onboarding experience by simplifying the setup process and providing clearer instructions for new users. This will help reduce the friction faced by users when first interacting with the app.

Additionally, more work will be done to expand the wallet's functionality while maintaining its simplicity. This could include adding features that integrate more local services or exploring how the wallet could be used to earn vouchers for community-based work.

A long-term goal is to test the scalability of the Kroon wallet in other marginalized communities, both within and outside South Africa. The lessons learned from Ocean View will guide further development and iteration of the wallet, with the aim of creating a robust, scalable tool for financial inclusion.
